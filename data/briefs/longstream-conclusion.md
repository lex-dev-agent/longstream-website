# Conclusion — Longstream Distillery

## Investigation Summary

This investigation assessed the viability of Longstream Distillery as a commercial craft gin business operating from a residential garage in Mangaroa, Upper Hutt, targeting NZ-wide sales with a goal of 100+ bottles per week.

## Key Findings

### Market: Competitive but viable
- NZ has ~200 distilleries. The market is crowded but still growing (projected 4.45% growth by 2027).
- Upper Hutt alone has 4+ distilleries (Wildkiwi, Enceladus, Aurora, Ruin), and the wider Wellington/Wairarapa region has many more (Reid + Reid, Lighthouse, Greytown, Southward).
- The native NZ botanical angle (manuka, horopito, kawakawa) is a genuine differentiator. Few distillers go as deep into native botanicals as a core range.
- Craft gin sweet spot is $60-80 NZD. Longstream's $69 RRP sits well.
- Target customers (25-40, experience-seeking, locally-minded) align with the product story.
- NZ-wide D2C online sales are the key to bypassing the crowded local market.

### Regulatory: Manageable but not trivial
- Three regulatory layers: CCA licence (Customs), off-licence (Upper Hutt City Council), food control plan (MPI).
- Estimated 4-6 months to full compliance, some steps running in parallel.
- The garage-as-CCA is the biggest unknown. Customs will inspect and may require security upgrades.
- Manager's Certificate needed. Someone must complete the training course.
- New energy labelling requirement from August 2025 must be factored into label design.

### Economics: Viable, with clear scaling path
- **Excise duty is $17.88 per bottle (38% ABV, 700ml).** This is the single biggest cost and is unavoidable.
- **Fermentation is already solved.** 14 vessels with 2 batches ready per day supports 100+ bottles/week with the current 25L still.
- The real bottleneck is still time and labour: each batch needs ~8 hours of still time and ~9.5 hours of active work.
- Cash cost per bottle (excl. labour): ~$27 at current scale.
- At $69 RRP with direct sales: 61% cash margin (owner-operated). This is healthy.
- Wholesale margins are thin (18% to bars/restaurants) due to the excise floor.
- **The 65L still reduces labour per bottle by 3x** (same hours, 3x output per batch), making the schedule far more manageable.
- **Automation drops active hours from ~9.5 to ~2 per batch**, making 2 batches/day viable as a side business.
- Capital investment of $7,800-12,300 pays back in 2-3 months at D2C pricing.
- Annual revenue potential at 108 bottles/week: $279K-373K depending on channel mix.

### Automation: Credible competitive advantage
- Ben's PLC/embedded/web background maps directly to the required tech stack (ESP32, MQTT, web dashboard).
- Phased approach (sensors, then automated cuts, then flow control, then fermentation management) is practical.
- Automation primarily solves the labour bottleneck, not the fermentation bottleneck (which is already addressed by the 14-vessel setup).

### Go-To-Market: Phase it
- Launch online D2C (NZ-wide) + Wellington region farmers markets for maximum margin.
- Expand to Wellington bars and restaurants once production is consistent.
- Scale to national distribution when volume warrants it.
- Instagram is the primary marketing channel. The engineer/maker story is genuinely interesting for local media.

## Risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| CCA licence rejected for residential garage | High | Early engagement with Customs Excise Officer. May need dedicated premises. |
| Resource consent required for commercial activity in residential zone | Medium | Check Upper Hutt City Council district plan. May need home occupation consent. |
| Home insurance complications | Medium | Disclose to insurer before commencing commercial production. |
| Excise duty increases | Low | CPI-linked annual adjustment. Budget for ~2-3% annual increase. |
| Market saturation / weak brand recognition | Medium | Focus on native botanical differentiation, NZ-wide online sales, and direct channels. Enter awards early. |
| Sugar wash perception | Low | Position as "distilled from scratch" rather than leading with the sugar wash base. Carbon filtering produces clean spirit. |
| Crowded Upper Hutt market | Medium | Don't position as "another Upper Hutt gin". Position as NZ-wide native botanical brand that happens to be made in the Hutt Valley. |
| Labour intensity at manual scale | Medium | Automation roadmap directly addresses this. 65L still is the highest-impact single investment for labour reduction. |

## Open Questions

1. **Upper Hutt zoning:** Does the district plan allow commercial distilling in a residential zone (Mangaroa)? If not, what consent is needed?
2. **Garage CCA suitability:** What specific security upgrades would Customs require? Worth an informal chat with a local Excise Officer before investing.
3. **Insurance:** What does the home insurer say about commercial spirit production on the property?
4. **Bottle size:** The brief mentions deciding between 700ml and 750ml. Recommendation: 700ml. It is the NZ/international standard for spirits and all competitor pricing benchmarks use it.
5. **Business structure:** Sole trader, partnership, or company? Tax and liability implications differ.
6. **GST registration:** Required once revenue exceeds $60K/year (likely within first year).

## Recommendation: Go

**The investigation supports proceeding with Longstream Distillery.**

The economics work, especially with direct sales. The fermentation setup is already scaled for 100+ bottles/week. The native botanical positioning is strong and differentiated. Ben's engineering background provides a genuine, uncommon competitive advantage in automation and scaling. The regulatory path is clear if time-consuming. The NZ-wide D2C strategy avoids over-reliance on the crowded Upper Hutt/Wellington local market.
